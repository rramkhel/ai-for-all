What to Put in Your Project

The checklist of things to add to your Claude Project, organized by industry.

Reading time: 30 minutes

What to Put in Your Claude Project

A checklist of artifacts that make AI actually know your business. Find your industry below, look at the list, start collecting. Each item has a colour: ● green safe to add as-is, ● yellow anonymize first, ● red leave out entirely.

The Quick Privacy Key

● GREEN — Safe to add directly. Your business information, your templates, your voice examples. Nothing identifying anyone else.

● YELLOW — Add it, but anonymize first. Strip names, addresses, exact amounts, contact details. Replace with [Customer A], "mid-range project," "approximately $45-55K," etc. The example email below shows you how.

● RED — Don't add. Belongs in your CRM, your accounting software, or a locked drawer — not in your AI.

Anonymizing an email — before and after

Before (● don't add this version):

Hi John, following up on the quote I sent you last Tuesday for the kitchen renovation at 142 Maple Drive. As discussed, the $47,500 includes the custom cabinets from Steve's shop. Let me know if your wife is comfortable with the timeline. — Mike

After (● safe to add as a voice example):

Hi [Customer], following up on the quote I sent you last week for the kitchen renovation. As discussed, the price includes the custom cabinets. Let me know if you're comfortable with the timeline. — [Me]

The voice is preserved. The identifying details are gone. That's the move.

Universal Items — Add These Regardless of Industry

Every Project, every business, every sector:

  • ● Your business basics — name, what you do, where you operate, how long you've been in business
  • ● Your voice rules — written in your own words. "I write the way I talk. Direct, plain, no fluff. I never say 'circle back' or 'reach out.' I sign emails with just my first name."
  • ● Who you serve — your typical customer or client in 2-3 sentences
  • ● Who you want more of — the kind of work or client you're trying to attract
  • ● What you don't do — things you turn away, scope you don't take. Saves AI from suggesting work you'd never accept.
  • ● Pricing structure — your rate card, packages, or fee schedule (your prices are your business, not your customer's data)
  • ● 3-5 of your best sent emails — pick ones in your voice that you'd be happy to receive yourself. Anonymize the customer names and specifics before adding.
  • ● 2-3 past customer success stories — what you did, the outcome. Anonymize identifying details.

Trades, Construction, & Skilled Services

Builders, electricians, plumbers, mechanics, cleaning, landscaping, anyone with trucks and tools.

  • ● Your service area — neighbourhoods, postal codes, how far you travel
  • ● The kinds of projects you take — kitchen renos, electrical service upgrades, lawn maintenance, etc.
  • ● Scope language you reuse — "we'll demo, frame, drywall, paint" — the standard phrases that show up in every quote
  • ● Standard inclusions and exclusions — what's always in your quote, what's always extra
  • ● Materials suppliers and standard products — Home Depot vs. specialty supplier, the brands you spec
  • ● Your trade certifications and credentials
  • ● Permit/inspection processes you handle in your jurisdiction
  • ● Warranty terms you offer
  • ● Payment structure — deposit, draws, final
  • ● 3-5 past quotes in your format — anonymize customer names and addresses
  • ● 3-5 sent customer emails — initial quote follow-up, scheduling, completion, the one where you handled a complaint well
  • ● 2-3 change order examples — anonymized
  • ● Customer payment details, banking, credit card info
  • ● Subcontractor pricing if it's negotiated confidentially

Restaurants, Cafés, & Food Service

Independent restaurants, cafés, food trucks, catering, anyone who feeds people.

  • ● Your menu — current version with descriptions, ingredients, allergens, prices
  • ● Your brand voice — warm/neighbourhood/storytelling, upscale/refined, fast-casual/punchy, etc.
  • ● Your story — how the place got started, the people behind it, what makes it different
  • ● Your neighbourhood/city context — what part of town, what's nearby, who walks in the door
  • ● Dietary specialties — vegan, gluten-free, halal, kosher, allergy-aware
  • ● Recurring specials and events — Tuesday pasta night, Sunday brunch, monthly wine dinners
  • ● 5-10 social posts that worked well — the ones that got engagement, in your voice
  • ● Your standard review-response approach — for both 5-star and 1-star
  • ● Suppliers and sourcing story — local farms, the cheese guy, the bread bakery
  • ● Catering packages and pricing
  • ● Hours, location, reservation policies
  • ● 2-3 past review responses you're proud of — anonymize the reviewer's name
  • ● 2-3 past email campaigns that worked — anonymize subscriber-specific content
  • ● Sample job postings for line cook, server, dishwasher
  • ● Employee personal info, scheduling specifics, payroll details
  • ● Supplier pricing if it's negotiated confidentially

Real Estate

Solo agents, small teams, listing focused or buyer focused.

  • ● Your farm areas — neighbourhoods, postal codes, building types you specialize in
  • ● Your specialty — luxury, first-time buyers, investors, downsizers, families — and the voice that matches
  • ● Your bio and credentials — designations, years in business, languages spoken
  • ● Your standard listing language — how you describe a kitchen, a yard, a finished basement
  • ● Your buyer/seller process documents — what you walk clients through
  • ● Standard scripts — open house follow-up, after-showing email, offer presentation
  • ● Fair housing compliance rules — explicitly: "never use language that implies preferences for or against protected classes" (add this as a rule in your Project Instructions)
  • ● Your brokerage's compliance/advertising rules
  • ● Common buyer questions and how you answer them — interest rates, closing costs, neighbourhood schools
  • ● Market context — what's been happening in your areas, typical price ranges
  • ● 5-10 past listings you wrote and were happy with — anonymize addresses
  • ● 3-5 after-showing follow-up emails — anonymize buyer names and addresses
  • ● Comp analysis approach — your method, with anonymized examples
  • ● Client pre-approval letters, financial information, SIN, banking details
  • ● Specific buyer motivations or vulnerabilities ("they have to sell because of divorce")
  • ● Seller bottom-line numbers they've shared with you in confidence

Mortgage & Insurance Brokers

Solo brokers, independent agents, financial advisors.

  • ● Your product lines — what kinds of mortgages, what kinds of policies, what carriers you work with
  • ● Your specialties — first-time buyers, self-employed, commercial, life insurance, group benefits
  • ● Your voice rules — formal, reassuring, transparent. Add explicit compliance guardrails: "never quote a rate without saying 'subject to underwriting'" or "always mention 'consult your advisor'"
  • ● Your explainer library — how you explain amortization, how you explain a deductible, how you explain underwriting. These are your most reused pieces.
  • ● Your renewal/retention approach
  • ● Standard process descriptions you explain to every client
  • ● Common objections and how you handle them
  • ● Regulatory framework you operate under (FSRA, AMF, etc.) so AI doesn't suggest non-compliant content
  • ● 5-10 past explainer emails — anonymize client specifics, keep your voice
  • ● Sample renewal outreach — anonymized
  • ● Sample marketing content — newsletter, social — anonymized
  • ● Client SINs, banking info, account numbers
  • ● Specific client financial details — income, debt, credit scores
  • ● Pre-approval letters, application packages with PII
  • ● Medical underwriting information for life/disability insurance

Solo Professional Services

Consultants, coaches, bookkeepers, accountants, HR consultants, recruiters, trainers, designers — anyone who sells expertise.

  • ● Your service offerings and packages — what you sell, at what price points
  • ● Your ideal client profile — the kind of work you want more of
  • ● Your brand voice — formal/conversational, technical/plain-English, warm/direct
  • ● Your methodology — frameworks you use, your approach to engagements
  • ● Your bio, credentials, and case studies (anonymized where needed)
  • ● Standard contract terms — what's always in your engagement letter
  • ● Standard templates — proposal structure, project plan, status report, invoice
  • ● Your LinkedIn recommendations and testimonials (these are public, fair game)
  • ● Your typical project lifecycle — from first call to final invoice
  • ● Things you don't do — projects you turn down, scope you won't take
  • ● 3-5 past proposals that won the work — anonymize client names, scope specifics
  • ● 3-5 sent client emails — proposal follow-up, kickoff, status update, handover
  • ● Past content in your voice — articles, posts, newsletters
  • ● For recruiters specifically: candidate writeup templates with [Candidate ID] not names; sample boolean searches; outreach templates
  • ● For coaches/therapists: be especially careful — client session content should generally stay out entirely
  • ● Specific client financial details, salaries, fee negotiations
  • ● Client confidential business info — strategy, M&A, personnel issues
  • ● For recruiters: candidate names paired with current employer + salary
  • ● For coaches/therapists: session notes, diagnoses, anything emotionally sensitive

Non-Profit & Charity

Executive directors, fundraising staff, program managers.

  • ● Your mission statement and theory of change
  • ● Your programs — what you do, who you serve, how you measure impact
  • ● Your voice and tone — mission-driven, donor-facing, beneficiary-respectful
  • ● Your impact data — beneficiaries served, outcomes, KPIs (the aggregate numbers, not individuals)
  • ● Your story library — the anecdotes you use in appeals (anonymized if they identify beneficiaries)
  • ● Your donor segments described generically — "major donors $5K+", "monthly sustainers", "lapsed donors"
  • ● Your standard reporting structures — board memo format, funder report format
  • ● Past funders and grant programs you've successfully accessed
  • ● Your fundraising calendar — appeal cycles, events, giving days
  • ● Your organization's history and milestones
  • ● 2-3 past grant proposals that won funding — anonymize any beneficiary-identifying content
  • ● 3-5 sent donor letters — appeal letters, thank-yous, stewardship updates. Anonymize donor names.
  • ● Sample board reports — anonymized
  • ● Sample volunteer onboarding materials — anonymized
  • ● Donor names paired with giving amounts
  • ● Beneficiary identifying information — names, addresses, specific circumstances
  • ● For health-adjacent non-profits (hospice, mental health, addictions): ANY protected health information stays out of consumer Claude. Period. Use Workspace Gemini with a signed BAA if you need AI on patient-adjacent work.
  • ● Personnel matters — performance reviews, disciplinary actions, compensation specifics

Retail, E-Commerce, & Product Businesses

Shop owners, online sellers, makers.

  • ● Your product catalogue — what you sell, descriptions, price points
  • ● Your brand story — how it started, what makes it different
  • ● Your brand voice — fun, premium, eco-conscious, irreverent, etc.
  • ● Your target customer — demographic, psychographic, where they hang out online
  • ● Your supplier story — sourcing, ethics, made-in-Canada angles
  • ● Your return/exchange/shipping policies
  • ● Past marketing campaigns that worked — emails, social, ads
  • ● Product photography style guide — if you have one
  • ● Your sales calendar — holidays, seasonal pushes, sale events
  • ● Past customer reviews in aggregate (anonymize reviewer names)
  • ● Sample customer service responses — anonymized
  • ● Past email campaigns — anonymized subscriber data
  • ● Customer payment information, account numbers
  • ● Customer addresses and contact info paired with purchase history
  • ● Supplier pricing if it's negotiated confidentially

How to Build Your Project — Right Now, In This Session

If you're following along on your own while we walk the room, here's the exact sequence. Should take about 30-40 minutes if you have your business stuff handy on your laptop.

Step 1 — Open a blank document (2 minutes)

Open Word, Google Docs, Notes, or whatever you normally write in. Save it as "My Business Brief — [Your Business Name]." This is your scratch pad. You'll paste the finished version into Claude in the last step.

Step 2 — Answer these questions in the doc (10 minutes)

Copy these questions into your document, then answer each one in 1-3 sentences. Don't overthink it — write the way you'd explain your business to a friend at a barbecue.

The questions:

  1. What is your business and how long have you been doing it?
  2. Where do you operate? (city, region, service area)
  3. Who is your typical customer or client? Describe them like you'd describe a regular.
  4. Who do you want MORE of? What kind of work or client are you trying to attract?
  5. What kind of work do you turn down or refuse?
  6. How would you describe the way you write or talk to customers? (formal? casual? direct? warm?) Give 2-3 specific examples of phrases you USE and 2-3 you NEVER use.
  7. What's your pricing or fee structure? (rates, packages, hourly, project-based)
  8. What's your process from first contact to finished job?
  9. What are the 3 most common questions customers ask you?
  10. What makes you different from your competitors? (in your own words, not marketing-speak)

That's your business brief. It's the most important file in your Project. Save it.

Step 3 — Hunt your files for examples (10 minutes)

Now look at the colour-coded checklist above for your industry. Pick 3 to 5 items from the green and yellow lists to add today. Don't try to find everything — pick the ones that would help you most this week.

For each item:

  • Find it — search your email, your Drive, your Documents folder, your invoicing software, your website. Wherever it lives.
  • Copy it — open a new document, paste in the contents (or save a copy of the file)
  • If it's ● yellow: delete customer names, addresses, phone numbers, exact dollar amounts. Replace with [Customer], [Address], "approximately $X-Y range." Look at the email example up top if you need a reminder.
  • Save with a clear title like "Past quote example — kitchen reno" or "Customer follow-up email — anonymized"

A short list to make this concrete — most people should grab some combination of:

  • A pricing sheet or rate card
  • One or two past quotes/proposals
  • Three or four past emails you wrote that sound like you
  • Your standard contract or terms
  • One or two case studies or success stories

Step 4 — Create your Claude Project (3 minutes)

On your laptop, go to claude.ai and sign in.

  1. Click "Projects" in the left sidebar
  2. Click "Create Project"
  3. Name it after your business (e.g., "Birchwood Builders" or "My Consulting Business")
  4. Add a short description if you want — one line is fine
  5. Click Create

You're now inside an empty Project.

Step 5 — Add your Project Instructions (5 minutes)

Look for the "Add instructions" or "Set custom instructions" button (it's usually on the right side or under a settings icon).

  1. Open the business brief document you made in Step 2
  2. Copy the entire thing
  3. Paste it into the Project Instructions field
  4. Add this line at the top: "You are my AI assistant for [business name]. Use the briefing below to help me with emails, drafts, quotes, and other work. Match my voice. Ask me clarifying questions when you need more context."
  5. Click Save

Your Project now has its "brain."

Step 6 — Upload your files to the knowledge base (5 minutes)

Look for the "Project knowledge" section (usually shows files you can drag in).

  1. Drag in the files you collected in Step 3 — your pricing sheet, anonymized quotes, anonymized emails, etc.
  2. Or click "Add content" and upload them one at a time
  3. Files can be Word docs, PDFs, text files, or you can paste content as text

Wait for them to upload. You should see them listed in the Project knowledge.

Step 7 — Test it (5 minutes)

Start a new chat inside your Project. Try a real prompt:

  • "Draft a follow-up email to a customer I quoted last week who hasn't responded yet."
  • "Write three Google Business Profile posts for this week."
  • "Help me explain [thing customers always ask about] in my voice."

Read the output. Does it sound like you? Does it use your pricing? Does it know your business?

If it doesn't sound right — that's not a failure, that's normal on attempt one. Tell Claude what's off: "That sounds too formal — I'm more casual. And we don't use the word 'leverage.' Try again." Then update your Project Instructions with what you just learned. The Project gets smarter every time you correct it.

When you walk out today, you'll have:

  • A Project named after your business
  • Instructions that tell Claude who you are and how you write
  • 3-5 real examples it can learn from
  • A first chat where you tested it

That's the foundation. Every week from now on, you add one more thing — an email that worked, a new template, an updated pricing sheet. By month three, it knows your business better than a new hire would after a year.

If you get stuck

We're walking around — wave us over. Common things people get stuck on:

  • Can't find the Projects button: make sure you're signed in to claude.ai (not the mobile app for this part)
  • Don't have past emails to use: that's fine — just write 2-3 paragraphs describing how you'd email a customer, in your own words. AI can learn voice from a description, not just examples.
  • Not sure if something is yellow or green: when in doubt, anonymize. Takes 30 seconds, removes the question.
  • Output sounds robotic: your voice rules need more specifics. Add 3-5 phrases you actually use and 3-5 you'd never use.

The Three Mistakes to Avoid

1. Trying to load everything at once. Don't spend a Saturday trying to upload your entire business. Five good examples beat fifty mediocre ones. AI learns from quality, not volume.

2. Putting in stuff you're not proud of. If a past email was rushed, awkward, or off-brand — don't add it. AI will mimic what it sees. Add the work you'd be happy to be judged by.

3. Forgetting to anonymize. Build the habit now while you're starting fresh. Easier to anonymize three emails on day one than to clean up a Project full of customer names six months from now.

When You're Done — How to Tell It's Working

You'll know your Project is set up right when:

  • You can ask "draft a follow-up email to [customer description]" and get back something that sounds like you wrote it
  • You stop having to re-explain your business at the start of every chat
  • Other people read what you produced and assume you wrote it yourself
  • You find yourself reaching for Claude before you'd normally sit down to write

If the output still sounds generic, the Project needs more voice examples. If it gets the facts wrong, the Project needs more business context. Keep feeding it. It compounds.

Print this. Find your industry. Cross things off as you find them. By the end of the session, your Project will know your business — and Monday morning, you'll have a real assistant on your laptop.
