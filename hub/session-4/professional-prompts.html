How to Write Professional Prompts

The methodology behind high-stakes professional prompts, with a worked legal research example.

Reading time: 20 minutes

How to Write High-Level Professional Prompts

The simple prompts in our Hub get you 80% of the way for everyday work. This guide is for the other 20% — when your work has real stakes, regulatory considerations, or needs to look like a senior professional wrote it. Read the anatomy below, then use the legal research example as a model for building your own.

THE MAIN POINTS

A great professional prompt is mostly NOT about the question. It's about the scaffolding around the question. The ratio is roughly:

  • 10% the actual question
  • 90% the scaffolding — role, rules, sources, format, refusals

That ratio is what separates "ChatGPT, summarize this" from output that looks like a junior associate's first draft.

The seven parts of a serious prompt

Every high-level professional prompt has these seven pieces:

  1. Role and stance — who is AI pretending to be, and what duties bind that role
  2. Accuracy and refusal rules — when to say "I don't know" instead of guessing
  3. Source hierarchy — what counts as authoritative, what doesn't
  4. Citation format — exactly how to attribute claims
  5. Verification instructions — how to check that claims hold up
  6. Output structure — the shape of the deliverable
  7. The actual case-specific question — usually the smallest section

Skip any of these and you're back to generic output.

The unbreakable rule: verify the links yourself

AI can confidently cite sources that don't exist, or that exist but say something different than claimed. Hallucinated citations have ended careers — a US lawyer was sanctioned in 2023 for filing a brief with six fake cases ChatGPT invented. The same risk applies to medical literature, financial regulations, historical claims, and technical specifications.

The rule: if AI cites a source, click the link and check the source yourself before using it. No exceptions. The prompt below has refusal rules built in, but those help — they don't replace verification.

ACTION STEPS — BUILD YOUR OWN PROMPT TEMPLATE (20 MINUTES)

If you're following along, here's how to build a high-level prompt for YOUR professional work. Don't try to do this with a stranger's domain — pick something you do regularly that has stakes.

Step 1 — Open a blank document (1 minute)

Save it as "My Professional Prompt — [task type]" (e.g., "My Professional Prompt — Engagement Letter Review" or "Client Memo Drafting" or "Grant Proposal Research").

Step 2 — Define the role (3 minutes)

Answer:

  • What kind of professional should AI act like? (a senior associate, a research analyst, a senior consultant — be specific)
  • Who reviews their work? (you, a client, a partner, no one)
  • What kind of mistakes would be most damaging in this work? (overpromising, missing nuance, citing bad sources, breaking confidentiality)

Write this as a paragraph at the top of your prompt. "You are acting as a senior [role] supporting [type of work]. Your output is reviewed by [whom] before [what happens to it]."

Step 3 — Set the accuracy rules (3 minutes)

Answer:

  • When should AI say "I don't know" instead of guessing?
  • What's the cost of a wrong answer? (in your field — discipline? lost client? bad outcome?)
  • What's the right thing to do when the answer is unsettled or split?

Write rules like:

  • "When you do not have reliable information on a point, say 'I cannot verify this — please confirm from primary source.'"
  • "When the [law/literature/data] is unsettled, identify the split rather than synthesizing a confident position."
  • "Hallucinated [citations / data / claims] are worse than acknowledged gaps."

Step 4 — Define what counts as a real source (3 minutes)

Answer:

  • What primary sources should AI cite? (specific databases, official websites, peer-reviewed journals, statute books — name them)
  • What secondary sources are OK as background but not authority? (textbooks, professional publications)
  • What sources should AI NOT cite at all? (blog posts, news summaries, AI-generated content from other tools)

This is the highest-leverage section. AI defaults to citing whatever's most easily searchable — which is often the lowest-quality option. Naming your sources changes everything.

Step 5 — Set the citation format (2 minutes)

Tell AI exactly how to attribute claims. Different fields have different standards:

  • Legal: McGill Guide (Canada) or Bluebook (US)
  • Medical/Scientific: AMA, APA, or Vancouver style
  • Academic: Chicago, APA, or MLA
  • Business/financial: depends on context — name the standard

If your field doesn't have a formal standard, write your own: "Cite sources inline as [Source Name, Date, link]. Include a sources list at the end. Don't cite anything without a link I can verify."

Step 6 — Spell out the output structure (3 minutes)

Answer:

  • What should every output look like, regardless of the specific question?
  • What sections does it need to have?
  • What's the right length?

For a research memo:

Produce output with these sections in this order: 1. Question, 2. Short Answer, 3. Applicable Law/Framework, 4. Application to Facts, 5. Adverse Views / Counter-Arguments, 6. Open Questions.

For a client deliverable:

Produce output with: 1. Executive summary (3-4 sentences), 2. Key findings (bulleted), 3. Recommendation, 4. Caveats and limitations.

Step 7 — Save it as a Project (5 minutes)

This whole document becomes the Project Instructions for a Claude Project named after the task (e.g., "Legal Research Memos" or "Grant Proposal Research" or "Client Diagnostic Reports").

From now on, every time you have a case/client/question of this type, you start a chat in that Project, paste the specific facts and question, and get back output that follows your scaffolding.

Built once. Reused forever. That's the unlock.

EXAMPLE — A FULL LEGAL RESEARCH PROMPT

The following is an example of all seven parts in one document. It's built for a Crown prosecutor doing case research, but the shape is what to copy. Look at the scaffolding, not the specific content.

After this example, there's a prompt you can use to adapt this template for YOUR profession.

THE EXAMPLE PROMPT

ROLE AND OPERATING STANCE

You are research counsel supporting a Crown prosecutor at the Alberta 
Crown Prosecution Service. Your output is research support, reviewed by 
Crown counsel before any use in case preparation, disclosure, or court. 
You are not counsel of record. You do not make charging decisions, 
sentencing recommendations, or strategic choices — those belong to Crown 
counsel.

You operate under the Crown's quasi-judicial duty: your job is to 
identify the law and the facts honestly, not to win. This means:

- Treat adverse authority with the same rigour as favourable authority. 
  Identify cases that hurt the Crown's position as carefully as those 
  that help it.
- Surface Charter issues proactively, even where defence has not raised 
  them.
- Flag any potential disclosure obligation under R v Stinchcombe and its 
  progeny that the facts might trigger.
- Where the law genuinely supports a defence argument, say so plainly. 
  Do not hedge to make the Crown's case look stronger than it is.

ACCURACY AND REFUSAL RULES

When you do not have reliable information on a point, say so explicitly. 
The exact phrase to use is: "I cannot verify this from my training data 
— please confirm directly via CanLII or another primary source before 
relying on this." A hallucinated case citation in a Crown brief is a 
discipline-level problem. Acknowledged gaps are not.

When the law is unsettled, say so explicitly. If appellate courts have 
split, identify the split and the leading authorities on each side. Do 
not synthesize a confident position out of conflicting authority.

If you are asked a question that requires you to take a position on 
prosecutorial discretion (whether to lay a charge, whether to proceed 
to trial, what sentence to seek, whether to enter a stay), refuse and 
explain that this is reserved to Crown counsel's judgment. You can 
identify the legal framework that informs those decisions, but you do 
not make them.

SOURCE HIERARCHY (USE IN THIS ORDER)

1. PRIMARY SOURCES — cite only these as authority:
   - Federal statutes: Criminal Code (RSC 1985, c C-46) and other federal 
     statutes accessible via laws-lois.justice.gc.ca
   - Provincial statutes: Alberta statutes via kings-printer.alberta.ca 
     and canlii.org/en/ab
   - Reported decisions: cite from CanLII (canlii.org) as the primary 
     access point. CanLII is the authoritative free database for 
     Canadian case law, statutes, and regulations across all jurisdictions 
     and court levels.
   - Where a Supreme Court of Canada decision is the leading authority, 
     cite the SCC neutral citation.

2. SECONDARY SOURCES — may be referenced as background, never as 
   authority, and must be clearly labelled as such:
   - Canadian Encyclopedic Digest (CED) on Westlaw
   - Halsbury's Laws of Canada
   - Treatises (e.g., Manning, Mewett & Sankoff for criminal; Stuart's 
     Charter Justice; Vauclair & Desjardins on criminal procedure)
   - Provincial Crown Practice Manuals where publicly available

3. DO NOT CITE AS AUTHORITY:
   - Law firm blog posts, summaries, or newsletters
   - Generic legal information websites aimed at lay readers
   - News reporting on cases (use to locate the underlying decision, 
     then cite the decision itself)
   - AI-generated summaries from other tools

CITATION FORMAT

Follow the Canadian Guide to Uniform Legal Citation (McGill Guide), 
9th edition. Specifically:

- Statutes: short title, statute volume, jurisdiction, year, chapter, 
  section. Example: Criminal Code, RSC 1985, c C-46, s 320.13(2).
- Cases: style of cause, neutral citation, parallel citation to a 
  reported source where available, paragraph number when quoting or 
  paraphrasing a specific holding. Example: R v Beatty, 2008 SCC 5 
  at para 43, [2008] 1 SCR 49.
- Where a case is available on CanLII and has no neutral citation, use 
  the CanLII citation. Example: R v Smith, 2019 ABQB 412 (CanLII).
- For unreported decisions, provide docket number and date.

VERIFICATION INSTRUCTION

Before stating that a case stands for a particular proposition:
- Confirm the holding has not been overruled or distinguished by a higher 
  court.
- Note any subsequent appellate treatment in Alberta specifically.
- If you are uncertain whether a case remains good law, flag it for 
  verification rather than stating it as settled.
- Where appellate treatment has shifted the analysis (e.g., the 
  post-Grant analysis of s. 10(b) detention; the post-Jordan framework 
  for s. 11(b) delay; the post-Bill C-46 dangerous and impaired driving 
  amendments), apply the current framework and note when it superseded 
  the prior one.

OUTPUT STRUCTURE

Produce a research memo with the following sections:

1. ISSUE
   - One-sentence statement of the legal question.

2. SHORT ANSWER
   - 2-4 sentences. The bottom line. Calibrate confidence honestly.

3. APPLICABLE LAW
   - Statute(s) with section numbers
   - Leading appellate authority with neutral citations
   - Current legal test, broken into its elements

4. APPLICATION TO THE FACTS
   - Walk through each element of the test against the facts provided.
   - Identify which elements are strongly established, which are 
     contestable, and which are weak.

5. ADVERSE AUTHORITY AND DEFENCE ARGUMENTS
   - The three cases most likely to be cited by defence on these facts.
   - The strongest defence argument and an honest assessment of its 
     prospects.
   - Any Charter issues the facts may engage, even if not raised.

6. DISCLOSURE FLAGS
   - Anything in the fact pattern that triggers a Stinchcombe-style 
     disclosure obligation Crown counsel should consider.

7. OPEN QUESTIONS / VERIFICATION NEEDED
   - Anything you are not confident about and recommend Crown counsel 
     verify directly.

THE FACTS OF THIS CASE

[Charge(s) and section(s) of the Criminal Code or other statute:]
[CHARGE]

[Anonymized accused descriptor — e.g., "the accused, a male in his 
mid-30s with no prior record"]:
[ACCUSED]

[Anonymized complainant descriptor where relevant:]
[COMPLAINANT]

[Date and location of alleged offence — general only, no street 
addresses or identifying detail:]
[WHEN AND WHERE]

[Summary of the alleged conduct, in neutral, factual terms:]
[ALLEGED CONDUCT]

[Police investigative steps relevant to the legal question:]
[INVESTIGATION]

[Statements made, with timing and whether Chartered/cautioned:]
[STATEMENTS]

[Physical or documentary evidence relevant to the legal question:]
[EVIDENCE]

[Defence positions communicated to date, if any:]
[DEFENCE POSITION]

THE RESEARCH QUESTIONS

[List specific questions in priority order]
[QUESTION 1]
[QUESTION 2]
[QUESTION 3]

ANONYMIZATION

The facts above have been anonymized for the purposes of this research. 
Do not attempt to identify the accused, complainant, or any other 
individual. If the facts as stated appear to identify a specific real-
world case, do not speculate about the identity — answer the legal 
question on the facts as given.

WHAT TO NOTICE ABOUT THE EXAMPLE

Six specific things this prompt does that most prompts don't:

1. The role section names the duties of the role, not just the title. "You are research counsel supporting a Crown prosecutor" is the title. The next paragraph — "you operate under the Crown's quasi-judicial duty: your job is to identify the law honestly, not to win" — is the duty. That second part is what shapes the output.

2. Refusals are scripted, not just permitted. The prompt doesn't say "feel free to say you don't know." It says "use this exact phrase: 'I cannot verify this from my training data — please confirm directly via CanLII.'" That precision is what gets used.

3. The source hierarchy is concrete. Not "use authoritative sources." Specifically: CanLII for cases, laws-lois.justice.gc.ca for federal statutes, kings-printer.alberta.ca for Alberta statutes. Named. Linked. Unambiguous.

4. There's an explicit "do not cite" list. Law firm blog posts, generic legal information sites, news reports. The prompt names the wrong sources so AI doesn't reach for them when the right ones are harder to find.

5. The output structure is enforced. Every output has the same 7 sections in the same order. Crown counsel never has to remember "what should this memo look like" — the prompt makes it identical every time.

6. Anonymization sits inside the prompt. The fact-pattern fields are explicitly labeled as anonymized. "Date and location — general only, no street addresses." It tells the user how to use the prompt as much as it tells AI how to respond.

ADAPT THIS TEMPLATE FOR YOUR OWN WORK

The example above is for legal research. Yours might be for medical literature review, financial analysis, technical writing, grant research, candidate assessment, or anything else where stakes are real.

To adapt it, you have two options.

Option A — Do it yourself

Take the seven sections from the example (Role, Refusal Rules, Source Hierarchy, Citation Format, Verification, Output Structure, Facts/Questions). Rewrite each one for your field. The action steps at the top of this document walk through how.

Option B — Have Claude adapt it for you

Paste this meta-prompt into Claude:

I'm building a high-level professional prompt template for my own work. 
Below is an example template built for a Crown prosecutor doing legal 
research. I want you to adapt this template's STRUCTURE and APPROACH 
for my profession.

My profession: [DESCRIBE YOUR WORK — e.g., "I'm a solo accountant who 
does corporate tax returns for small businesses" or "I'm an executive 
recruiter doing candidate assessments" or "I'm a non-profit ED doing 
grant proposal research"]

The kind of work I want the template for: [DESCRIBE — e.g., "drafting 
candidate writeups for clients" or "researching foundation grants my 
non-profit might qualify for"]

The stakes of getting it wrong: [DESCRIBE — e.g., "I could send a 
client a writeup with the wrong salary band" or "I could waste hours 
applying for a grant we're not actually eligible for"]

Here is the original template. Adapt each of its seven sections for 
my profession and my specific use case. Keep the structure. Change 
the content.

[PASTE THE ENTIRE LEGAL RESEARCH PROMPT FROM ABOVE]

For each section, ask me clarifying questions if you need information 
I haven't provided. Don't guess about my profession's standards — ask 
me. When you're done, produce the adapted template as a single 
ready-to-use prompt I can save into a Claude Project.

That meta-prompt will walk you through the adaptation. Claude will ask you about your professional sources, your citation standards, your output structure, and your refusal rules. By the end, you'll have a template tailored to your field that you can save into a Project and reuse on every case.

THINGS TO WATCH OUT FOR

Three honest warnings:

1. Verify every link AI cites

A scripted refusal rule helps but doesn't eliminate hallucinated citations. The most professional-sounding prompt with the most carefully named sources can still produce a citation that doesn't exist or that says something different than claimed. Click every link. Read the source. Confirm the holding.

If you're a lawyer, doctor, financial advisor, or anyone whose work has regulatory exposure — your name goes on the work, not Claude's. Verify everything.

2. The prompt doesn't replace your judgment

A well-built prompt produces well-structured first drafts. It doesn't produce final decisions. Whether to lay a charge, what diagnosis to give, what investment to recommend, what scope to take on — those belong to you. The prompt's job is to give you a starting point for your judgment, not a substitute for it.

3. Update the prompt when the world changes

Laws change. Best practices shift. Source databases get reorganized. The prompt you built in May 2026 may need updating by May 2027. Build a habit: every few months, re-read your Project Instructions and update anything that's gone stale.

IF YOU GET STUCK

We're walking around — wave us over. Common things people get stuck on:

  • Don't know what counts as a primary source in my field: ask a more senior person in your profession, or check a professional association's research guide
  • My field doesn't have a formal citation standard: make your own — pick a format and stick to it
  • The output structure feels arbitrary: start with what you wish a junior person would produce. That's your output structure.
  • The whole thing feels like a lot of work: it is, the first time. Maybe 30-60 minutes. Then you save it as a Project Instructions and use it forever. The math works out.

Build it once. Reuse it forever. That's the difference between people who try AI and people who use AI.
